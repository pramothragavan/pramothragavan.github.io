A Demonstration of Ramsey's Theorem

Ramsey’s theorem intuitively states that in a large enough set, there must be some amount of order. To state this formally, suppose that $k$ and $l$ are given positive integers. Ramsey’s theorem asserts the existence a positive integer $N$ such that for $n\ge N$, if the 2-subsets of an $n$-set are coloured red or blue in any manner, there is either a $k$-set all of whose 2-subsets are red, or an $l$-set all of whose 2-subsets are blue.

It’s worth mentioning that colouring a 2-set can be visualised as colouring the edge in a graph induced by the $n$-set. The Ramsey number $R_2(k,l)$ is defined as the smallest number for which the conclusion of the above theorem holds.

Finding exact values of Ramsey numbers is one of the most difficult open problems in combinatorics. For instance, $R_2(k,k)$ is not known for $k>4$. Paul Erdős made the point this way. If aliens invaded the earth and gave us a year to calculate $R_2(5,5)$, he said, we could put all our mathematicians and computers on the job and probably succeed. But if they had asked instead for $R_2(6,6)$, our only hope would be to destroy them before they destroyed us.

Below is a toy demonstration of Ramsey’s theorem. You are welcome to set values for parameters $n$, $k$ and $l$, and a random colouring of the complete graph on $n$ vertices will be generated. If a blue $k$-set or a red $l$-set exists, then it will be highlighted (note that if you are using the dark theme, then pink and purple are used instead). Of course, the existence of a clique does not mean that you have found the value of $R_2(k,l)$ (as we would need to show a clique exists for all colourings, not just a random one), but the non-existence of such a clique does correspond to a lower bound. Ramsey’s theorem can be generalised further by allowing for more than 2 colours, and allowing for colouring subsets of any size. The latter generalisation makes it a little harder to visualise things.

Theorem: Let $k, r, l_1, \dots, l_r$ be given positive integers with $k\leq l_i$ for $i=1,\dots, r$. Then there exists a number $n$ with the following property: if the $k$-subsets of $\{1,…,n\}$ are coloured with $r$ colours $c_1,\dots, c_r$, then for some value of $i \in \{1,\dots,r\}$, there exists a set of $l_i$ points, all of whose $k$-subsets have colour $c_i$. The smallest such $n$ is denoted $R_k(l_1, l_2, \dots, l_r)$.

There is in fact an infinite version of Ramsey’s Theorem:

Theorem: Given positive integers $k$ and $r$, if the $k$-element subsets of an infinite set $X$ are coloured with $r$ colours, there is an infinite monochromatic subset $Y$ of $X$.
